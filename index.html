Abstract

The SENSE ontology models building energy systems in their operation phase. For that purpose it aggregates further ontologies that all together enable a full description of a building at metadata level [1]. The resulting ontology set is illustrated on the right as an interactive network. Clicking on a circle will open the specific documentation of the ontology. Additional ontologies consist of the Energy System Information Model (ESIM), Metrics, Risk and BAF models.
  • MM: Metrics Model → specification view, tree view
  • RISK: Risk Ontology → specification view, tree view
  • BAF: Building Automation Functions → specification view, tree view
  • ESIM: Energy System Information Model → specification view, tree view
All together, they allow for a full desciption of a building, its technical systems incl. HVAC and BACS, sensors and meters, potential faults as well as required monitoring and control tasks for an energy-saving operation. In that sense, it is not only a product description but it models also the process of monitoring and controlling buildings. ESIM describes the built-in energy system at building and district level. The Metrics model consists of a set of quantities and KPIs (Key Performance Indicator) defining metrics that are useful for HVAC engineers. The Risk model provides a catalog of possible faults and operation errors that can lead to energy wastes, together with corrective energy conservation measures. The BAF model is a catalogue of generic control functions usually implemented by automation engineers. Finally, the Sense Ontology represents the central knowelge graph that aggregates the several concepts and provides a complete semantic digital twin of a building.

Introduction back to ToC

In the field, several metadata schemas have emerged and are for a part reused in the ontology system. Among them it is important to mention ifcOWL released by buildingSmart as an OWL (Web Ontology Language) representation of IFC (Industry Foundation Classes) [2]. The automation domain was formally described into SSN/SOSA [3] and CTRLont [4]. The Brick ontology [5] has emerged a few years ago for representing HVAC systems. The Building Topology Ontology (BOT) describes the topological structure of buildings [6], and the QUDT ontologies were initiated by the Constellation Program at NASA [7]. By contrast, SENSE represents a set of Ontologies that together can fully describe building energy systems integrating different domains and concepts for e.g. HVAC, BACS, risk, monitoring tasks, operaton rules, energy saving..., and thus support automating Building Energy System Monitoring and Control. It is meant to conceptualizes the process anf tasks involved in building operation. That way, it shall support configuring, deploying and operating building energy management systems (BEMS), FDD systems and further building energy services.

SENSE: Description back to ToC

SENSE is a semantic composition of some ontologies. It is itself an ontology that is not only a product description of a building energy system, but it formalizes also the process of monitoring and controlling buildings. ESIM describes the built-in energy system at building and district level. The Metrics model consists of a set of quantities and KPIs (Key Performance Indicator) defining metrics that are useful for HVAC engineers. The Risk model provides a catalog of possible faults and operation errors that can lead to energy wastes, together with corrective energy conservation measures. The BAF (Building Automation Functions) model is a catalogue of generic control functions usually implemented by automation engineers. Finally, the Sense Ontology represents the central knowelge graph that aggregates the several concepts and provides a complete semantic digital twin of a building.Sense ontology represents the central knowledge model that aggregates all the concepts, and in which logical axioms and rules are encoded for formalizing expert knowledge. This contrasts with the other ontologies that focus on system description while the Sense ontology aims at emulating the process of BMS setup by characterizing the system and prescribing applicable monitoring functions
